Mobile Loan Products in Kenya

Kenya's mobile money sector has attracted widespread international attention for transforming the financial opportunities of the country's unbanked and underbanked population. The country has developed a thriving market in mobile loans: instantly approved personal loans, usually unsecured, managed via mobile phone. The loans are typically $15 - $20 in value, with a 30-day repayment term and a fee ranging from 2% to 10% of the loan amount. In Kenya's economy, mobile loans serve niches filled in other markets by credit cards, payday loans, checking account overdrafts and revolving lines of credit. The three-year CAGR in the personal lending sector in Kenya is estimated at 18% by BPI Bank's equity research service [1]. This high rate of growth suggests that suppressed demand is quickly giving way to a broadening of financial inclusion. Between November 2012 and December 2015, mobile lenders disbursed total loans valued at more than $820 million dollars [2].

Most mobile loan products in Kenya have thus far retained a pricing structure characteristic of payday loans, namely short repayment terms with high annual percentage rates (APRs). The fees of the top Kenyan mobile lenders generate APRs ranging from 24% to 120% [3]. Nevertheless, interest rates that intuitively seem high are not necessarily unjustified if the loans are priced to serve customer segments with correspondingly high levels of credit risk. Conventional payday lenders argue that high rates are necessary to offset the costs of high-risk borrowers and high operating costs due to storefront overheads, customer acquisition and loan processing costs. How can we evaluate whether the high APRs seen in Kenya really are justified, or whether there are signs of overpricing? One way would be to look at the sector's non-performing loan (NPL) rates as well as the returns being generated. If mobile loans are earning disproportionate returns compared to comparable financial services, this would tend to confirm overpricing in the market. Since financial reports specific to this segment are not generally published, we can use a model to estimate lender's gross margin and other performance results based on typical features of the mobile loan products reported in Kenya.

Low NPLs Suggest Effective Risk Management

The model illustrates the interplay of commercial factors that determine lender profitability in this type of business: interest rates and NPL rates, risk management through credit scoring, customer mix between new and repeat borrowers. Because Kenya's mobile loan services generally cut off credit at the first instance of non-repayment, the model reflects the fact that repeat borrowers tend to have lower default rates than new customers while driving up transaction volumes. On the cost side, the model's measure of gross margin ensures that COF and loan losses are accounted for. It does not include non-interest operating expenses, as there was no data on which to base an estimate, or taxes. As a result the model is most useful for assessing whether interest rates are priced high enough to cover loan losses given customer mix, while suggestive of whether margins are likely to be in line with industry norms.

Where financials are available, Kenyan banks involved in mobile lending have reported NPL rates of 2% - 6% and RoAA of 4% - 5% (see table below). However, most of these indicators represent aggregate performance across all bank activities and don't apply directly to assessments of mobile loan pricing. Only the reports for M-Shwari and KCB MPESA are specific to the mobile loan segment.

Now we turn to our simulation. The model suggests that a product with an average loan fee of 7.5%, 4% COF, initial credit score accuracy of 95%, and an average of 3.2 loans per customer per year, could achieve an NPL rate of 2.9% and earn a gross margin of about 55%. Increasing the interest rate to 10%, all else being equal, yields a gross margin of 88%. Meanwhile, reducing the interest rate to 2% yields a negative gross margin (loss) unless the initial credit score accuracy is increased from 95% to at least 97.4%. Although not definitive, these results suggest that returns for mobile loans priced at the mid- to upper end of the range in Kenya may be higher than normal for this type of financial service.

One conclusion that can be drawn from the model is that it would be very difficult to achieve the low overall NPL rates reported by the leading mobile lenders in Kenya without high accuracy levels in the credit scoring algorithms for new customers. Low NPLs suggest that the market leaders have succeeded in managing the risk of mass market penetration at scale under current economic conditions. This includes periods of relative economic weakness and increasing KBRR rates as was seen during the latter half of 2015 [4]. With lending risk contained in such a way, there is a question of why APRs of up to 120% are justified. If lenders were serving classes of customers with initial risk levels above 5%-10%, we would expect NPLs to be higher.

Where pricing levels should ultimately land can't be argued definitively without more information on lenders' costs, but we can look to other markets for examples. In the United States, credit card delinquency rates have ranged from 2.11% to 6.81% over the past ten calendar years [5]. These delinquency rates are similar to the NPLs seen in Kenya. Over the same ten years, average APRs have ranged from 12.74% to 15.24% [6]. In the Kenyan loan pricing structure, this would be equivalent to mobile loan fees of 1.1% to 1.3%. Meanwhile, U.S. credit cards generally remained profitable: pre-tax returns generated by large U.S. credit card banks ranged between 2.4% and 7.7% during the period, other than 2009 at the height of the financial crisis when the industry return dropped to -5.33% [7]. While I wouldn't expect Kenyan rates to fall to these levels any time soon, it is likely that rates can go down with competition. Alternatively, the model suggest that current loan pricing of 7%-10% monthly could support riskier customer segments while maintaining returns consistent with industry averages.

Conclusions

This analysis suggests that profit margins in Kenya are healthy for lenders that have cracked the credit scoring problem. Some products are priced high relative to reported NPLs, especially for the lowest-risk customers. Lower rate options exist for those who make the effort to shop around, and the market is attracting new entrants who will drive price and feature competition over time. Market indicators tracking repeat borrower behavior and cumulative interest paid would help regulators, consumer protection analysts and financial education providers respond to market trends in more targeted and effective ways.

The low NPL rates evidenced in Kenya thus far have demonstrated that the risks of mass market lending in a lower middle income country can be successfully managed. The combination of data-driven behavioral credit scoring algorithms, attention to user experience management, the use of scalable digital delivery channels, and institutional arrangements to track loan defaulters has proven to be a successful model. The transparency and accessibility of mobile money payment systems in Kenya have attracted both domestic banks and foreign venture-backed lenders to enter the market. Over time, mobile lenders that survive will enhance competition and help price levels become better differentiated among risk classes. The market is already showing signs of this process.
